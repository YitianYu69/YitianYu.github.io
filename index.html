Biography

A detailed narrative about my background, interests, and growth.

I am Yitian Yu, a Computer Engineering student at the Georgia Institute of Technology with a strong interest in the technical foundations that make modern computing possible. My interests grew from a broad curiosity about how intelligent systems work into a more focused commitment to building efficient machine learning systems. What excites me most is the way electrical and computer engineering connects theory with real implementation. In one setting, that means reasoning about algorithms, numerical stability, and optimization. In another, it means thinking about memory, throughput, hardware constraints, and how software decisions interact with system design.

At Georgia Tech, I have been intentionally developing both depth and range. On the depth side, I spend significant time studying machine learning, computer vision, distributed training, and model efficiency. I enjoy reading papers, rebuilding methods from scratch, and understanding why a method works instead of only using it as a black box. On the range side, Computer Engineering keeps me grounded in the broader stack. My coursework and projects have kept me engaged with programming, systems, and hardware-oriented thinking, which has made me more thoughtful about performance and resource constraints. That combination is one of the biggest reasons I chose this major: it allows me to approach AI not only as a modeling problem, but also as a systems problem.

A major part of my growth has come from independent technical exploration. I like to test ideas, compare implementations, and follow the details until I can explain the tradeoffs clearly. Over time, this habit has shaped how I learn. I do not want to stop at surface-level understanding. I want to know how data flows through a system, where bottlenecks appear, why optimization becomes unstable, and how design choices affect training behavior. This mindset has pushed me toward projects involving PyTorch, distributed training, model architecture design, and efficiency-focused experimentation. It has also helped me become more comfortable with debugging, iteration, and long-form technical work.

Professionally, I am especially interested in AI systems that make strong models more practical. I am drawn to work involving training efficiency, compression, hardware-awareness, and robust engineering pipelines. In the long term, I want to contribute to the development of machine learning infrastructure and research systems that help advanced models scale efficiently and responsibly. I am interested in roles such as machine learning engineer, research engineer, or systems-focused AI developer, especially in environments where research and implementation are closely connected.

Personally, I am motivated by steady growth. I value technical rigor, but I also value reflection. This portfolio is partly a record of accomplishments, but it is also a way for me to understand my own direction more clearly. It helps me connect past work with future goals. It also reminds me that progress is rarely linear. Strong engineering work usually comes from repeated testing, revision, and learning from failure. That process has become one of the most meaningful parts of my education.

This website is intended for professors, peers, recruiters, and collaborators who want to understand what I am building and what I care about. It highlights my resume, career goals, and project work, but it also communicates a broader theme: I want to become an engineer who can bridge ideas and implementation. Whether I am working on training pipelines, computer vision models, optimization methods, or future research questions, I want my work to be thoughtful, technically grounded, and useful in real settings.

Technical Project Samples

The core of my ePortfolio and the section that will continue to grow over time.

Efficient Training and Activation Compression

One of my major technical interests is making deep learning systems more efficient without losing their effectiveness. In this line of work, I have explored activation compression, hardware-aware training, and memory-efficient techniques for modern vision models. My goal has been to understand how to reduce training cost while still preserving useful learning dynamics. This kind of project requires both algorithmic thinking and systems awareness, since the best solution is rarely only about accuracy or only about speed. It is about the tradeoff between the two.

Through this project, I have worked with PyTorch-based training pipelines, custom optimization experiments, and implementation details related to large-scale model training. I am especially interested in how ideas such as lower-bit representations, better initialization, and structured training strategies interact with convergence and resource usage. The broader value of this work is that it connects theory with real engineering constraints. For future employers and collaborators, it demonstrates my ability to take a complex systems problem, analyze bottlenecks, and build toward a more practical solution.

Computer Vision Modeling and Experimentation

I have also spent substantial time on computer vision projects involving convolutional neural networks, training pipelines, and empirical evaluation. These projects helped me move beyond simply using standard models and toward understanding architecture choices, optimization behavior, and experimental design. Instead of treating performance as a single number, I focus on how and why a model behaves the way it does: which changes improve stability, which changes only affect short-term accuracy, and which ideas generalize across settings.

This work strengthened my ability to build end-to-end machine learning experiments. That includes setting up training code, choosing reasonable baselines, recording results carefully, and analyzing tradeoffs. It also improved my technical communication because meaningful project work depends on being able to explain methods, assumptions, and conclusions clearly.
